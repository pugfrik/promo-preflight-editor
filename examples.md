# Examples — what good critique looks like

Three complete reviews, in the exact output format `rules.md` requires.
Study the shape: findings quote the draft's own words, name an operational
consequence, and never fix anything. (Section headings appear one level
deeper here only to nest inside this file — in a real review, use the
`##`-level headings exactly as `rules.md` specifies.)

---

## Example 1 — a bad promo (legal lens correctly silent)

**The draft:**

> "40% off all appetizers during Friday happy hour, 5–7pm. Goal: see if
> more people come in. We'll promote on social."

**The editor's critique:**

### What will sink this promo

1. **"See if more people come in" is not a goal you can score.** Compared
   to what? Without the trailing 8-Friday baseline for that 5–7pm window,
   the promo ends with a feeling, not a verdict — busy Fridays happen
   without promos too.
2. **You're discounting the people who were already buying.** Friday 5–7pm
   is a peak window; 40% off apps applies to every existing order, so your
   best case — a full room — is also your most expensive. What's the
   contribution margin on an appetizer at 40% off?
3. **"We'll promote on social" is unowned work.** Who writes the post, who
   schedules it, who answers the comments asking if it includes takeout?
   "We" is nobody, and the promo launches silent.
4. **Nothing here says how a redemption gets counted.** If the discount is
   rung as a generic comp, Friday's report can't separate promo orders
   from normal ones — the data to judge this dies at the register.

### Answer before you run this

- What did the 5–7pm window gross on the last 8 Fridays (covers and
  appetizer sales)?
- What is the food cost on your appetizer menu, and what margin is left
  at 40% off?
- Who owns the social posts, and what's the posting schedule?
- How will the POS tag a promo appetizer vs. a regular one?
- Is this one Friday, a month of Fridays, or open-ended?

## Example 2 — a decent promo (praise earned; findings anyway)

**The draft:**

> "Tuesday Trivia Night, 7–9pm, starting Sept 2. $5 entry per team
> donated to the local food bank; winning team gets a $25 gift card.
> Sarah owns setup + hosting; we'll track covers vs the last 8 Tuesdays
> on the POS."

**The editor's critique:**

### What's strong

A named owner (Sarah), a defined window, and a pre-declared baseline —
covers vs. the last 8 Tuesdays — put this ahead of most drafts before it
runs. The measurement thinking is real.

### What will sink this promo

1. **$5 entry + a prize is a consideration smell.** Paying to enter a
   game with a prize can read as an illegal lottery in some forms even
   when the fee goes to charity; trivia's skill element helps, but that's
   a legal judgment, not an operator's call (US-SWEEP-01 smell) — verify
   with counsel before running this.
2. **The gift card is an open loop.** What can the $25 card buy? If it's
   redeemable against alcohol, the prize is effectively discounted
   alcohol (NY-ABC-05 smell) — verify with counsel before running this;
   what the card may buy is currently undefined anywhere in the draft.
3. **Covers measure attendance, not the promo's economics.** Trivia teams
   camp tables for two hours; if they nurse one drink each, covers go up
   while revenue per seat goes down. Track Tuesday gross and per-seat
   spend alongside covers, or a "win" may cost you money.
4. **Sarah owns setup and hosting — who owns overflow?** If twelve teams
   show up in a room that seats eight comfortably, what's the plan —
   turn people away, or a waitlist? Success without a cap is a staffing
   problem on your quietest-staffed night.

### Answer before you run this

- Where does the $5 go mechanically — your till then a donation, or a
  collection box? (The answer changes the legal shape.)
- What may the $25 gift card be redeemed for?
- What was average per-seat spend on the last 8 Tuesdays, 7–9pm?
- What's the team cap, and what happens to team thirteen?

## Example 3 — an alcohol giveaway (legal lens fires)

**The draft:**

> "Win free beer for a year! Every purchase this month is an entry.
> Winner drawn Oct 1, announced on Instagram."

**The editor's critique:**

### What will sink this promo

1. **The prize is alcohol — that's the one thing you can't give away.**
   Free alcohol is broadly barred for licensed venues, and "free beer for
   a year" is free alcohol twelve months running (NY-ABC-02 smell) —
   verify with counsel before running this.
2. **"Every purchase is an entry" is the classic lottery trap.** Prize +
   chance + purchase-as-consideration is the illegal-lottery triad; a
   free, equal-odds way to enter (or dropping the purchase tie entirely)
   is the known cure, and it must be practical, not buried (US-SWEEP-01 /
   US-SWEEP-02 smell) — verify with counsel before running this.
3. **A public drawing with no official rules mentioned.** Sponsor,
   eligibility (21+ at an alcohol venue, even for non-alcohol prizes),
   entry period, odds, prize value — none are drafted, and "announced on
   Instagram" isn't a rules document (US-SWEEP-03 smell) — the rules
   list is a requirement, counsel reviews it once.
4. **"Worked" is undefined beyond a pile of entries.** Entries measure
   participation, not lift. What number moves this month — gross, new
   customers, sign-ups — and what's its baseline from the trailing
   same-month history?

### Answer before you run this

- What non-alcohol prize could carry the same excitement? (That decision
  reshapes the whole legal profile — make it before talking to counsel.)
- Is there a free entry path with odds equal to a purchaser's?
- Who drafts the official rules, and who reviews them?
- What metric defines success for the month, against what baseline?
