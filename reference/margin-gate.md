# The margin gate — does success actually pay?

Used by the **Margin** lens. This is a hard pass/fail question, asked of
every promo before it runs:

> **What's the base margin on the product — and if this offer succeeds,
> does it net enough margin to do the work at the quality we want, for as
> long as it runs, without trapping the owners in manual labor?**

An offer that "works" but consumes owner time it can't pay for is a
**losing offer even at high volume**. (The source lesson: a roofer feeling
stretched thin has a symptom; the *problem* is an offer whose margin can't
pay for the talent to replace himself.)

## The questions behind the gate

- What is the contribution margin per redemption AFTER the discount or
  giveaway cost?
- At full uptake — the promo's best case — what does total margin look
  like against the total labor to deliver it?
- Whose hours deliver this? If the answer is the owner's, what is the
  offer paying for those hours?
- Can the volume be capped by design (fixed prize count, off-hours only,
  limited redemptions) so the downside is bounded?

## Calibration examples

- **Free product sampling during open hours** = *good* free offer: staff
  already present, sampling already offered, one-time setup work —
  near-zero marginal owner labor, pure new-customer recruitment.
- **Win-a-free-event giveaway** = *conditional*: profitable only for the
  right customer types. If the business earns on per-unit sales during
  the event, event types whose guests don't buy (e.g., events where the
  crowd doesn't drink, at a bar) lose money — more bookings of those
  means more work for no return. Rank the event/customer types by what
  they actually spend before steering the promo at them.
